01 12 Years of Godot

From a modest 326 PRs in 2014 to over 7,000 per year by 2024. The 2020–2022 surge reflects Godot 4.0 development, while 2022–2023 saw an influx driven by the Unity pricing controversy pulling developers toward the open-source alternative.

02 The Merge Clock

The median PR is reviewed and merged in just 2 days — but the mean jumps to 24.5. That gap tells a two-speed story: a fast lane of routine fixes, and a long-tail of complex features that take months or years to land.

71.6% of all merges happen within the first week
6,167 PRs took longer than a month to merge
545 PRs waited over a full year before being merged
03 The Contributor Funnel

Recognition matters. First-time contributors achieve a 27% merge rate, while established collaborators land 93% of their PRs. Core members consistently push through the highest volume, forming the engine's backbone.

04 The Review Economy

AThousandShips alone accounts for 1,885 reviews — nearly double the next contributor. The top 5 reviewers handle 40% of all reviews. More striking: only 6.6% of review submissions are formal approvals, revealing a culture of discussion over verdict.

Top 5 reviewers handle 40% of all reviews — a significant bus-factor risk
89.2% of reviews are COMMENTED — discussion without a formal verdict
714 Approvals vs 9,573 comments — formal sign-off is rare
05 Community Wishlist

These open PRs have the most community reactions — work people are actively waiting for. GDScript Traits leads with 468 reactions. The list is a window into Godot's most-wanted features that haven't yet made it to main.

06 Label Universe

Enhancement and bug reports are nearly equal — 27,548 vs 26,961. The editor and core subsystems dominate the topic labels, while platform coverage spans Android, Windows, macOS, and Linux at comparable volumes.

07 The Aging Backlog

Of the 4,961 currently open PRs, some date back to 2019. The chart reveals not just volume but the vintage of deferred decisions — design questions that have remained open for years, waiting for the right moment, reviewer, or consensus.

08 Codebase DNA

C++ (.cpp + .h) accounts for 73% of all file changes — Godot is fundamentally an engine built in C++. The modest GDScript (.gd) and C# (.cs) numbers reflect that most contributors work at the engine layer, not the scripting layer.

09 The Size Penalty

Tiny PRs (≤10 lines) merge at 78% — nearly double the rate of huge PRs (≤50%). The pattern is consistent and actionable: smaller, focused changes are dramatically more likely to land. Note: based on PRs with detail data fetched.

10 The Legends

Calinou leads with nearly 3,000 PRs over a decade — an astonishing sustained output. The top 20 contributors account for a disproportionate share of the project's history, yet each brings a distinct focus area to the engine.

11 The Global Clock

PR submissions peak at 13–17 UTC — European afternoon, US East morning — revealing where the contributor base is concentrated. Activity never falls silent: even at 3am UTC, hundreds of PRs are opened each hour.

12 Codebase Hotspots

The editor and scene subsystems see the most PR traffic. The thirdparty/ directory shows modest PR count but outsized code churn — it's vendored dependencies being updated in bulk, not organic development.

13 Die Fast or Live Forever

30% of PRs resolve on the day they're submitted. Half are gone within 3 days. But the curve flattens sharply after 30 days — PRs that survive a month tend to persist for months or years, becoming the aging backlog we saw in section 07.

14 What Travels with "bug"?

Of 26,980 bug-labeled PRs, 30% also carry topic:editor — making the editor the single biggest source of reported bugs. regression appears on 1-in-8, and crash on 1-in-14, quantifying the severity tail of the bug backlog. Platform labels reveal that Windows and Android surface proportionally more bugs than other platforms.

15 The Bug Lift Chart

Lift measures statistical association — how much more (or less) likely a label is on a bug PR versus any PR at random. crash and regression land at 2.05× — genuinely twice as predictive of bugs as the baseline. Meanwhile feature proposal (0.015×) and topic:codestyle (0.20×) are almost never bugs. Notably, topic:editor looks dominant in raw counts but its lift of 1.06× reveals it's just the biggest topic — not disproportionately buggy.

crash · regression both sit at ~2.05× lift — the clearest bug signals
topic:input · topic:gdscript · topic:rendering all elevated above 1.25×
feature proposal has lift 0.015× — essentially never a bug
16 Bug Association Map

Plotting volume (x, log scale) against lift (y) separates four quadrants: top-right is high-frequency and strongly bug-associated; bottom-right is common but not distinctive; top-left is rare but a strong signal when it appears; bottom-left barely interacts with bugs at all. Point size scales with total label usage across all PRs.

19 Merge Speed Over Time

Is the project's review process scaling with growth? This chart tracks median and mean merge time per year alongside PR volume. A widening gap between median and mean signals a growing long tail — more PRs getting stuck for months while the fast lane stays fast.

20 Time to First Review

21 Review Iterations

25 Contributor Stickiness

26 Stale PR Predictors

What correlates with a PR still being open after 1+ years? PR size is a strong predictor — huge PRs are dramatically more likely to go stale. This is actionable: encourage contributors to split large changes into smaller, reviewable pieces.
